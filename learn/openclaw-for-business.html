OpenClaw for Business:
AI Employees Are Replacing VAs

Your VA costs $3-5K a month. Works 8 hours a day. Needs training, management, and sick days. There's a better way.

I'm going to say something that might sound harsh: most virtual assistants are doing work that an AI agent can handle better, faster, and for a fraction of the cost.

Not all VA work. But a lot of it. The repetitive stuff. The inbox management. The CRM updates. The research. The scheduling. The follow-ups.

That's exactly what OpenClaw agents are built for.

The real cost of a VA

Let's do honest math. A decent VA costs:

  • Offshore VA: $1,500-2,500/month for full-time
  • US-based VA: $3,000-5,000/month
  • Specialized VA (executive assistant): $4,000-7,000/month

Plus the hidden costs nobody talks about:

  • Training time: 2-4 weeks before they're productive
  • Management overhead: You spend 30-60 min/day managing them
  • Turnover: Average VA tenure is 6-12 months. Then you start over.
  • Time zones: If they're offshore, coordination eats into your day
  • Quality inconsistency: Bad days, distractions, mistakes

Over a year, a $3K/month VA costs you $36,000+ in salary alone. Add management time and that number climbs fast.

What an OpenClaw agent can replace

Here are real tasks that businesses are shifting from VAs to OpenClaw agents:

  • Morning briefings: Scan inbox, CRM, calendar. Deliver a daily summary at 7am.
  • CRM management: Auto-update deal stages, log interactions, score leads
  • Email triage: Categorize, prioritize, draft responses for review
  • Research: Competitor monitoring, market research, prospect research
  • Follow-ups: Automatically draft and queue follow-up messages
  • Scheduling: Coordinate meetings, manage calendar conflicts
  • Reporting: Pull data, generate weekly summaries
  • Content drafting: Blog posts, social media, email newsletters

What VAs still do better

I'm not going to pretend AI replaces everything. VAs are still better at:

  • Phone calls — AI voice isn't there yet for complex conversations
  • Relationship management — Nuanced human interactions
  • Physical tasks — Obviously
  • Highly creative work — Strategy, brand voice development, novel problem-solving

The smart move isn't replacing your VA entirely. It's replacing the 60-70% of their work that's repetitive, and letting them focus on the high-value stuff. Or just cutting the VA entirely if most of their work falls in the "automatable" category.

Real example: my setup

I run a SaaS called DMpro. My OpenClaw agent (Jars) handles:

  • Morning briefings every day at 7am
  • CRM updates and lead scoring
  • GitHub repository backups
  • Overnight code builds
  • Competitor research
  • Website deployments
  • Calorie and fitness tracking

Before Jars, I was considering hiring a VA for $3K/month. Instead, I spent about $1,500 on the setup and $100-150/month on server and API costs. That's a savings of roughly $30,000 per year.

And the agent works 24/7. It doesn't forget things. It doesn't need vacation.

How to make the switch

You don't need to go cold turkey. Here's the smart approach:

  1. Audit your VA's tasks. List everything they do in a week.
  2. Categorize. Which tasks are repetitive and rule-based? Those are automatable.
  3. Start with one workflow. Pick the biggest time sink and automate it first.
  4. Measure results. Is the agent doing it as well or better? Cheaper?
  5. Scale. Move more tasks to the agent over time.
